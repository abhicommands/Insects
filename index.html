Field Journal: The Uninvited Guest

Location: Backyard patio, Plainsboro, NJ

Date: March 28, 2025 — 6:12 PM — Humid, 67°F

Insect Observed: Mosquito (Culex pipiens or possibly Aedes albopictus)

The first warm evening of spring brought more than just the scent of blooming forsythia — it brought mosquitoes. I felt that familiar sting on my ankle before I even saw it. When I finally spotted the tiny, almost weightless body hovering near my calf, I remembered how much this insect — seemingly insignificant — has shaped human history.

As I sat still, I watched it land, its needle-like proboscis piercing my skin with surgical precision. It fed quickly and silently, leaving only a slight itch behind. A pest, sure — but an incredibly efficient one. The female, in need of a protein-rich blood meal to lay eggs, shows a kind of ruthless evolutionary drive I can’t help but admire.

Mosquitoes have always hovered at the edge of my life. One of my relatives in Nepal — my mom's cousin — caught dengue during a bad outbreak in Kathmandu a few summers ago. It was a scary time. Everyone in their neighborhood was covering up water containers, burning coils, and checking windows obsessively. He recovered, but barely — his platelet count had dropped dangerously low. Since then, every mosquito buzz feels a little more ominous.

It’s strange — for such tiny creatures, they leave a big psychological imprint. As a kid, I’d lie awake listening to their high-pitched whines, too scared to move. My grandma used to say if you hear one, it’s already too late.

Now, as someone who studies insects, I’m trying to reframe how I see mosquitoes. Yes, they’re vectors of disease, but they’re also marvels of adaptation — drawn to CO₂, body heat, and sweat. The one I saw tonight probably hatched from a puddle near the gutter, ready to continue a cycle that’s lasted millions of years.

I wonder if we’ll ever really beat them — or if we’ll just keep learning to live around them.
